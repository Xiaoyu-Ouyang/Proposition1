\section{Background and Motivation}

Molecular spin dynamics is central to interpreting experiments in which the measured signal is generated by the time evolution of nuclear or electronic spin degrees of freedom, rather than by static molecular structure alone.
Quantum chemistry can provide microscopic parameters for effective spin Hamiltonians, including scalar $J$ couplings, hyperfine tensors, dipolar couplings, exchange interactions, $g$ tensors, and zero-field splittings.
Spin-dynamics simulation then converts these parameters into experimentally measurable time-domain signals, spectra, correlation functions, and coherent spin-state populations \cite{Hogben2011Spinach,Ledbetter2009ZeroFieldJSpectra}.

Examples occur across magnetic resonance, spin chemistry, and molecular quantum materials.
In liquid-state and zero-field NMR, nuclear spins evolve under chemical shifts, scalar $J$ couplings, and residual spin--spin interactions. The resulting free-induction decay or magnetization signal is Fourier transformed into spectra whose multiplet structure can serve as a molecular fingerprint \cite{Theis2013ChemicalAnalysis,andrewsDFTassistedNaturalAbundance2026}.
In EPR and ENDOR, unpaired electron spins and nearby nuclear spins produce spectra governed by $g$ tensors, hyperfine couplings, and zero-field splittings \cite{Stoll2006EasySpin}.
Radical-pair systems provide a different electron-nuclear example: two unpaired electron spins coupled to nuclear spin baths undergo coherent singlet-triplet mixing, while the subsequent reaction yields require additional open-system terms \cite{Antill2024RadicalPy}.
Molecular magnets and molecular spin qubits further motivate the study of coupled electronic spin networks and their nuclear-spin environments, where coherence, control, and relaxation determine possible quantum-technology applications \cite{Coronado2020MolecularMagnetism}.
Driven polarization-transfer processes such as dynamic nuclear polarization are also important, but they typically involve pumping and dissipation and therefore lie outside the central scope of this proposal.

These examples can be organized along two useful axes.
By the origin of the spin degrees of freedom, one distinguishes nuclear-only spin networks, electron-nuclear spin systems, and coupled electron-spin networks.
By the dynamical structure, one distinguishes isolated coherent Hamiltonian evolution, driven dynamics, and open-system processes such as relaxation, recombination, and pumping.
This proposition focuses on the first dynamical class: isolated coherent spin dynamics generated by effective spin Hamiltonians on molecular coupling graphs.

Experimentally, spin dynamics is usually observed indirectly.
These measurements are powerful, but the observed signals generally require a Hamiltonian model for interpretation.
Dense many-spin multiplets can be difficult to assign, individual couplings or graph effects are hard to isolate experimentally, and exact numerical simulation becomes exponentially expensive as the spin network grows.
Numerical spin dynamics therefore provides the missing bridge: starting from quantum-chemistry-derived spin-Hamiltonian parameters, it predicts finite-time observables, spectra, and correlation functions, and it allows controlled tests of how molecular graph structure affects spin evolution.
