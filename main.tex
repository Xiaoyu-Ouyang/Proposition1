\section{Central Claim}
Use tree graph tensor network and belief propagation to simulate the large scale spin dynamics in quantum chemistry.

\section{Background and Motivation}

Molecular spin dynamics is central to interpreting experiments in which the measured signal is generated by the time evolution of nuclear or electronic spin degrees of freedom, rather than by static molecular structure alone.
Quantum chemistry can provide microscopic parameters for effective spin Hamiltonians, including scalar $J$ couplings, hyperfine tensors, dipolar couplings, exchange interactions, $g$ tensors, and zero-field splittings.
Spin-dynamics simulation then converts these parameters into experimentally measurable time-domain signals, spectra, correlation functions, and coherent spin-state populations \cite{Hogben2011Spinach,Ledbetter2009ZeroFieldJSpectra}.

In this proposal, the concrete motivating example is zero-field nuclear magnetic resonance (ZF NMR), where the measured spectrum is generated primarily by coherent nuclear-spin evolution under scalar $J$ couplings.
For a liquid-state molecule in the absence of a strong magnetic field, the leading spin Hamiltonian is
\begin{equation}
H_{\rm ZF}=2\pi\sum_{i<j}J_{ij}\mathbf I_i\cdot\mathbf I_j+\sum_i \gamma_i \mathbf B_{\rm res}\cdot\mathbf I_i+H_{\rm small},
\end{equation}
where $J_{ij}$ are through-bond scalar couplings, $\mathbf B_{\rm res}$ denotes residual magnetic fields, and $H_{\rm small}$ may include weak residual dipolar, quadrupolar, or instrumental perturbations.
The initial state is usually not a pure state, but a high-temperature mixed state prepared by prepolarization in a magnetic field,
\begin{equation}
\rho_0\simeq \frac{\mathbf 1}{d}+\epsilon\sum_i\gamma_i I_i^z,
\end{equation}
which is then allowed to evolve after transfer to near-zero field.
The measured free-induction signal is a time-dependent magnetization, for example
\begin{equation}
M_\alpha(t)=\mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\rho(t)\sum_i\gamma_i I_i^\alpha\right],
\end{equation}
and its Fourier transform gives a molecular fingerprint spectrum determined by the $J$-coupling network \cite{Theis2013ChemicalAnalysis,andrewsDFTassistedNaturalAbundance2026}.
Typical scalar couplings in small organic molecules range from a few hertz to hundreds of hertz, so chemically relevant ZF NMR spectra usually lie in the hertz-to-kilohertz range and require coherent evolution over milliseconds to seconds.
This makes ZF NMR a natural target for testing tensor-network spin dynamics: the Hamiltonian is molecular, graph-structured, and approximately isolated on the coherent time scale, while the observable is directly a finite-time response signal.

Experimentally, spin dynamics is usually observed indirectly.
These measurements are powerful, but the observed signals generally require a Hamiltonian model for interpretation.
Dense many-spin multiplets can be difficult to assign, individual couplings or graph effects are hard to isolate experimentally, and exact numerical simulation becomes exponentially expensive as the spin network grows.
Numerical spin dynamics therefore provides the missing bridge: starting from quantum-chemistry-derived spin-Hamiltonian parameters, it predicts finite-time observables, spectra, and correlation functions, and it allows controlled tests of how molecular graph structure affects spin evolution.


\section{Existing Numerical Methods}

The most direct approaches to molecular spin dynamics propagate the spin system in either Hilbert space or Liouville space.
Hilbert-space methods, including exact diagonalization, Krylov or Lanczos propagation, and Chebyshev expansions, apply directly to arbitrary coupling graphs and provide controlled benchmarks for small spin networks.
Their cost grows as $\prod_i(2I_i+1)$, which becomes $2^N$ for $N$ spin-$1/2$ particles, so they cannot serve as a general large-system method.
Magnetic-resonance experiments often require mixed-state observables, so practical simulations frequently propagate the density matrix or product-operator expansion in Liouville space, where the dimension scales as the square of the Hilbert-space dimension.
Spinach is one practical implementation of this Liouville-space viewpoint, combining density-matrix propagation with sparse algebra and magnetic-resonance-specific state-space restrictions \cite{Hogben2011Spinach}.
For example, Andrews \textit{et al.} used quantum-chemical $J$ couplings to predict ZF NMR spectra by propagating the density matrix in time with Spinach to generate free-induction decays, and by diagonalizing the same ZF Hamiltonian in the frequency domain to obtain transition frequencies and amplitudes \cite{andrewsDFTassistedNaturalAbundance2026}.
Restricted-state-space, cluster, and semiclassical methods can reach larger spin systems by discarding weak many-spin correlations or using problem-specific physical approximations \cite{Kuprov2011PolynomialStateSpace,Yang2008ClusterCorrelation,Lindoy2014ImprovedSemiclassical}.
These methods are indispensable references, but their scalable versions rely on truncation assumptions whose accuracy must be checked case by case, especially when dense molecular coupling networks generate many comparable coherences.
This motivates complementary tensor-network approaches, where the approximation is controlled by bond dimension and by the chosen network topology.

The most mature tensor-network framework for real-time many-body dynamics is based on matrix product states (MPS), together with algorithms such as time-evolving block decimation, time-dependent density matrix renormalization group, Krylov-MPS propagation, and the time-dependent variational principle \cite{Schollwock2011DMRGMPS,Paeckel2019MpsTimeEvolution}.
These methods are highly effective for one-dimensional and quasi-one-dimensional systems, where local Hamiltonians admit compact matrix-product-operator representations and the relevant bipartite entanglement can be captured by moderate bond dimensions.
Molecular spin Hamiltonians, however, are naturally defined on coupling graphs rather than on paths.
Applying an MPS therefore requires choosing a one-dimensional ordering of the spins and embedding the molecular coupling graph into a chain.
If strongly coupled or strongly correlated spins are far apart in this ordering, graph-local interactions become long-range terms in the chain representation, increasing the matrix-product-operator bond dimension and the cost of time evolution.
The same ordering can also create unfavorable entanglement cuts: a single MPS bond may need to carry correlations generated by many molecular graph edges crossing that one-dimensional bipartition.
Spatial, coupling-strength, mutual-information, and graph-spectral orderings can reduce these bottlenecks by placing strongly interacting or strongly correlated sites closer together \cite{Legeza2003OptimizingDMRGOrdering}.
Nevertheless, these heuristics cannot remove the constraint that the tensor-network topology is a 1D path.

This limitation motivates tree and graph tensor-network representations, which relax the one-dimensional path constraint and allow the network connectivity to better reflect branching or modular structures in the molecular coupling graph.
Tree tensor network states (TTNS) are the simplest such generalization. 
Replacing the MPS path by a loop-free graph changes the available bipartitions and can distribute correlations across several branches rather than forcing them through a single chain bond \cite{nakataniEfficientTreeTensor2013}.
In quantum chemistry ground-state calculations, TTNS and related tree ansatzes have been developed as generalizations of DMRG for strongly correlated electronic structure problems, including variable-order TTNS and three-legged tree tensor network states \cite{nakataniEfficientTreeTensor2013,Murg2015VariableOrderTTNS,Gunst2018T3NS}.
These studies show that tree-like molecular correlation structures can require fewer renormalized states than MPS, although the larger tensor degree and prefactors make the practical advantage system-dependent.
Time evolution with tree tensor networks has also been developed in broader many-body settings, including early tree-network algorithms and later time-dependent variational principles for finite loop-free tensor networks \cite{Shi2006Classical,Bauernfeind2020TDVPTTNS}.
What remains less established is a systematic use of graph-adapted tree or message-passing tensor networks for molecular spin Hamiltonians in quantum chemistry, where the target observables are finite-time signals, spectra, and correlation functions rather than only ground energies.
This gap motivates the central question of our proposal, that whether a tensor-network topology adapted to the molecular coupling graph can improve real-time spin dynamics accuracy at fixed computational cost, especially relative to a one-dimensional MPS representation.

\section{Tree Graph Tensor Network Belief Propagation Method}

Belief propagation provides a natural contraction language for tensor networks whose spatial connectivity is a tree.
For tree graphical models and tree tensor networks, BP gives an exact contraction structure; it becomes an approximation only when the graph contains loops or when the messages themselves are compressed \cite{yedidiaUnderstandingBeliefPropagation2003,alkabetzTensorNetworksContraction2021}.
Recent influence-functional BP methods use this idea for quantum dynamics by representing each directed spatial message as a temporal tensor network that summarizes the dynamical influence of one branch on the rest of the system \cite{Park2025IFBP}.
This is closely related to earlier tree tensor network dynamics and time-dependent variational principles on loop-free tensor networks \cite{Shi2006Classical,Bauernfeind2020TDVPTTNS}.

In a Trotterized spacetime tensor network on a finite tree, each spin site contributes a local temporal tensor or temporal matrix product operator \(T_i\), and each directed edge \(i\to j\) carries a temporal MPS message \(m_{i\to j}\).
This message represents the whole branch attached to \(i\) after cutting the edge \((i,j)\).
The ordinary density-matrix message update contracts the local tensor with all incoming messages except the one from the target direction,
\begin{equation}
m_{i\to j}\simeq
\mathcal C_{\mathrm{RDM}}\!\left[
T_i\prod_{k\in \partial i\setminus j}m_{k\to i}
\right],
\end{equation}
where \(\partial i\) denotes the neighbors of \(i\), and \(\mathcal C_{\mathrm{RDM}}\) compresses the outgoing temporal MPS as a standalone object.

Our Research Summary tested an environment-dependent version of this finite-tree propagation.
Reduced-transition-matrix (RTM) compression forms the same candidate outgoing message, but chooses its truncated subspace using the reverse message \(m_{j\to i}\) as the contraction environment,
\begin{equation}
m_{i\to j}\simeq
\mathcal C_{\mathrm{RTM}}\!\left[
T_i\prod_{k\in \partial i\setminus j}m_{k\to i};
m_{j\to i}
\right].
\end{equation}
Operationally, all directed messages are first initialized by ordinary RDM compression using a leaf-to-root pass followed by a root-to-leaf pass.
The RTM calculation then performs bidirectional environment-dependent sweeps, updating messages toward the root and away from the root until the compressed message equations reach a fixed point.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{treeFiniteAmplitude.png}
    \caption{Finite-tree message propagation benchmark from the Research Summary.
    The test computes the Loschmidt amplitude on a depth-4 binary transverse-field Ising tree with \(h_z=0.6\), \(h_x=-1.0\), and initial state \(\ket{\mathbf 0}\).
    At the same temporal message bond dimension, RTM compression gives a smaller amplitude error than ordinary density-matrix compression, supporting the use of environment-dependent message compression on tree geometries.}
    \label{fig:treeFiniteAmplitude}
\end{figure}

The finite-tree result in Fig.~\ref{fig:treeFiniteAmplitude} is not yet a molecular spin calculation, but it establishes the algorithmic ingredient needed for this proposal.
On a tree, BP does not introduce an additional loop approximation, so the main numerical question is whether finite-bond-dimension temporal messages can accurately represent branch influence functionals.
The observed RTM improvement supports the principle that a message should be compressed according to the contraction in which it will be used, rather than according to its standalone norm.
The proposed work will test whether this advantage survives for quantum-chemistry-derived molecular spin Hamiltonians, where the interactions are generally anisotropic, nonuniform, and defined on coupling graphs that are not exactly trees.

The first concrete algorithmic task is to stabilize and accelerate the RTM message fixed-point iteration.
For compression schemes that treat each outgoing message independently, such as ordinary density-matrix compression, fitting-based compression, direct compression, SRC, or zip-up compression, tree message propagation is usually a single directional contraction with no nonlinear environment-consistency problem.
RTM is more accurate because it chooses the message subspace using the reverse message as an environment, but this also makes the message equations mutually dependent and can require repeated sweeps.
Direct inversion in the iterative subspace (DIIS) is a natural way to stabilize such fixed-point iterations by extrapolating from previous message residuals \cite{pulayConvergenceAccelerationIterative1980,pulayImprovedSCFConvergence1982}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{bpTmSequentialDiis.png}
    \caption{Preliminary DIIS test for the same depth-4 binary-tree transverse-field Ising benchmark as Fig.~\ref{fig:treeFiniteAmplitude}, measured at a fixed final time.
    Sequential BP-TM updates without DIIS show visible oscillations before reaching the fixed-point amplitude, while DIIS stabilizes the approach to the same converged value.
    Damping also stabilizes the iteration but approaches the plateau more slowly, and the manually swept RTM update provides a reference for the sweep-based implementation.}
    \label{fig:bpTmSequentialDiis}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:bpTmSequentialDiis} suggests that DIIS is useful primarily as a stabilization tool for environment-dependent RTM updates, rather than as a black-box guarantee of faster convergence in every case.
For molecular spin graphs, the tree will generally be irregular rather than a perfect binary tree, and local observables need not require globally converging every message with equal effort.
A practical implementation should therefore combine DIIS with asynchronous or importance-weighted message updates, focusing computational effort on branches whose RTM residuals remain large or whose messages have the strongest influence on the target local observable.

The second concrete task is to choose tree geometries for molecular spin Hamiltonians and validate whether those geometries improve the finite-time dynamics.
This problem does not arise in the binary-tree TFIM benchmark, where the Hamiltonian graph is already the same tree used by the tensor network.
Molecular spin Hamiltonians are different in two ways.
First, the interaction is not generally Ising-like; in ZF NMR the dominant coupling is the scalar interaction \(J_{ij}\mathbf I_i\cdot\mathbf I_j\), while more general molecular spin Hamiltonians can contain anisotropic bilinear couplings.
Second, the molecular coupling graph is usually weighted, nonuniform, and loopy rather than a tree.
The tree tensor-network geometry must therefore be constructed from the molecular Hamiltonian or from approximate information about the evolving state.

Ground-state TTNS calculations in quantum chemistry provide a useful precedent for this problem.
When no correlated wavefunction is available, a common strategy is to use a Hamiltonian-based proxy for orbital correlation, such as the exchange integral \(K_{ij}\), and choose a tree that keeps strongly coupled orbitals close \cite{nakataniEfficientTreeTensor2013}.
For a candidate tree \(T\), this can be written as a weighted tree-distance objective,
\begin{equation}
\min_T \sum_{i<j} w_{ij} d_T(i,j),
\end{equation}
where \(d_T(i,j)\) is the number of tree edges between sites \(i\) and \(j\), and \(w_{ij}\) is the strength or correlation weight assigned to the pair.
In DMRG and related tensor-network ground-state calculations, more state-informed orderings can instead use orbital mutual information \(I_{ij}\), when a preliminary correlated calculation is available \cite{Legeza2003OptimizingDMRGOrdering,Murg2015VariableOrderTTNS}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{qcTreeConstructionFig9.png}
    \caption{Example of tree construction in quantum-chemistry TTNS ground-state calculations, adapted from Nakatani and Chan \cite{nakataniEfficientTreeTensor2013}.
    The left panel represents an orbital graph weighted by exchange integrals \(K_{ij}\), and the right panel shows an approximate degree-bounded tree that keeps strongly interacting orbitals close while avoiding high-degree tensors.
    This motivates the analogous problem in molecular spin dynamics: construct a tree from a weighted spin-coupling graph and then test whether that tree improves finite-time observables.}
    \label{fig:qcTreeConstruction}
\end{figure}

For molecular spin dynamics, we will test three concrete tree-construction strategies.
The first and primary strategy is Hamiltonian-weighted construction.
For scalar-coupled nuclear spins, we set \(w_{ij}=|J_{ij}|\).
For a general bilinear interaction \(H_{ij}=\mathbf S_i^{\mathsf T}A_{ij}\mathbf S_j\), we set \(w_{ij}=\|A_{ij}\|_F\).
This construction is cheap because the weights come directly from the quantum-chemistry-derived spin Hamiltonian, and it is the closest spin analogue of the \(K_{ij}\)-based tree in Fig.~\ref{fig:qcTreeConstruction}.

The second strategy is initial-correlation construction.
When the initial state contains meaningful pair correlations, we can set
\begin{equation}
w_{ij}=I_{ij}(0)=S_i(0)+S_j(0)-S_{ij}(0).
\end{equation}
For high-temperature ZF NMR initial states, however, the full density matrix is close to the identity plus a small deviation density matrix, so \(I_{ij}(0)\) may be weak or uninformative.
In that case, initial deviation-density correlations or target-observable two-point correlations may be a more useful initial-state diagnostic than literal full-density-matrix mutual information.

The third strategy is short-time dynamical-correlation construction.
Here a low-cost pilot calculation, such as exact evolution on small systems or short-time MPS evolution, estimates which spin pairs become dynamically correlated before the target simulation time.
One possible weight is the time-averaged pair mutual information,
\begin{equation}
w_{ij}=\frac{1}{T_0}\int_0^{T_0} I_{ij}(t)\,dt.
\end{equation}
For mixed-state spin dynamics, a more robust option may be the time-averaged connected correlation relevant to the measured observable,
\begin{equation}
w_{ij}=\frac{1}{T_0}\int_0^{T_0} |C_{ij}(t)|\,dt.
\end{equation}
This construction is more expensive and risks circularity, because it uses a preliminary dynamics calculation to choose the tree.
We will therefore treat it as a diagnostic or adaptive refinement, rather than as the default tree construction.

The validation will ask whether these increasingly informed trees reduce the actual bottlenecks in the BP dynamics.
For each molecular spin Hamiltonian, we will compare random trees, one-dimensional MPS orderings, Hamiltonian-weighted trees, initial-correlation trees, and short-time dynamical-correlation trees.
At fixed message bond dimension and comparable computational cost, the primary observables will be finite-time magnetization or FID error, spectrum error after Fourier transform, message discarded weight, and the maximum or average entropy across tree edges.
For a tree edge \(e\) that partitions the sites into \(A_e\) and \(\bar A_e\), one diagnostic is
\begin{equation}
S_e(t)=-\mathrm{Tr}\,\rho_{A_e}(t)\log \rho_{A_e}(t),
\end{equation}
or the corresponding message entropy in the mixed-state or operator-space calculation.
The key test is whether lower weighted tree-distance or weighted-cut cost predicts lower message complexity and smaller observable error.
If the short-time dynamical-correlation tree gives only marginal improvement over the Hamiltonian-weighted tree, the Hamiltonian-weighted tree will be preferred because it is cheaper and directly available from the molecular spin Hamiltonian.
